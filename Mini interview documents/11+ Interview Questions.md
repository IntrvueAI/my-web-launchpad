# 11+ Interview: The Main School Interview
*Top Independent School Level (St Paul's, Westminster, King's Wimbledon and similar) — Spoken Interview Format*

---

## Purpose & Assessment Criteria

This is the whole-child interview. It assesses four core competencies, **not** rehearsed answers:

- **Articulate Self-Awareness** — can the child speak clearly and honestly about their own interests and values?
- **Reasoning Under Pressure** — can they think aloud, tackle the unfamiliar, and explain their thought process?
- **Independent Curiosity** — do they show genuine interest in the world, books, ideas and challenges?
- **Moral Maturity** — can they reason about fairness, empathy and right vs wrong without being prompted?

The interviewer moves **dynamically between four sections** — Personal, Reasoning, Extracurricular, and Current Affairs & Ethics — reading the child and choosing what to ask next. Warmth over interrogation. Effort and honesty are rewarded; polish and bragging are not. There are rarely single "right" answers — what matters is the thinking.

---

## SECTION A — Personal Insight

*Confidence, communication and honest self-awareness. Look for specific stories over generalities, and reflection over rehearsal.*

---

### A1 — Tell Me About Yourself

> "To start us off — tell me a bit about yourself. Whatever you'd like me to know."

**What a strong response looks like:** A calm, natural answer that picks two or three genuine things and gives a specific example for at least one ("I like building things — last month I made a…"). Comfortable, conversational, not a list of achievements.

**What a weak response looks like:** A rehearsed CV read aloud ("I play the violin, I do chess club, I'm in the football team…") with no example or feeling, or a one-line answer with nothing to follow.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Which of those matters most to you, and why?"*
- *"What would your best friend say you're like?"*

**What to listen for:** Can they be specific and reflective rather than generic? A child who says *why* something matters to them is showing self-awareness. Gently steer away from bragging toward honesty.

---

### A2 — Most Proud Of

> "What's something you've done recently that you're genuinely proud of? It doesn't have to be a big thing."

**What a strong response looks like:** A concrete moment, often small, with a reason it mattered — effort, improvement, or helping someone. "I finally got a handstand after weeks" beats "I won a trophy" if the reflection is richer.

**What a weak response looks like:** A prize or grade stated with no story, or "I don't know / nothing."

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"What was the hardest part?"*
- *"What did you learn about yourself?"*

**What to listen for:** Do they value effort and growth, or only outcomes? Reflection on the *process* is the mark of maturity here.

---

### A3 — A Time You Failed

> "Tell me about a time something didn't go the way you hoped. What happened, and what did you do next?"

**What a strong response looks like:** Honest ownership of a setback, no blaming, and a sense of what they took from it or how they responded. Comfort discussing imperfection.

**What a weak response looks like:** "I've never really failed," or a humble-brag failure ("I only came second"). Defensiveness or blaming others.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Would you do anything differently now?"*
- *"How did you feel at the time — and afterwards?"*

**What to listen for:** Resilience and honesty. Being able to sit with failure without needing to look perfect is exactly the poise top schools want.

---

### A4 — What Makes You, You

> "What do you think makes you a bit different from other people your age?"

**What a strong response looks like:** A thoughtful, specific trait or interest, ideally with an example, offered without arrogance. Even "I'm quite quiet but I notice a lot" shows self-knowledge.

**What a weak response looks like:** "I'm the best at…", or "I don't know, I'm just normal" with no attempt to reflect.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Is that something you like about yourself?"*
- *"When did you first notice that about you?"*

**What to listen for:** Genuine self-knowledge delivered with humility. Steer away from comparison/bragging and toward honest description.

---

### A5 — A Difficult Decision

> "Tell me about a time you had to make a tricky choice. How did you decide?"

**What a strong response looks like:** A real dilemma, the factors they weighed, and honest reasoning — even if the choice was small (which friend to sit with, whether to own up to a mistake).

**What a weak response looks like:** A choice with no weighing of options, or an invented, over-tidy story.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"What did you have to give up by choosing that?"*
- *"Are you happy with how it turned out?"*

**What to listen for:** Do they consider more than one factor, and can they live with trade-offs? Early signs of judgement.

---

## SECTION B — Reasoning & Thinking Aloud

*Logic, lateral thinking and hypotheticals. The answer matters far less than the process. Reward willingness to try, structured thinking, and calm under pressure.*

---

### B1 — Everyone Forgets How to Read

> "Here's a strange one. Imagine that tomorrow, everyone in the world suddenly forgot how to read. What would happen? Talk me through your thinking."

**What a strong response looks like:** A structured exploration — who's affected (children, doctors, drivers, governments), a chain of consequences, and comparison to how we manage now. "First, signs and instructions stop working, so… then…". Considers both problems and how people might adapt.

**What a weak response looks like:** A one-line answer ("It would be bad") or random guessing with no structure.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Who would be affected first?"*
- *"Would anything actually get better?"*

**What to listen for:** Structure and stamina. A child who names stakeholders and follows a chain of effects is reasoning well. Reward effort and calm even if they wobble.

---

### B2 — The Fair Way to Share

> "You have 8 sweets to share between 3 children, but you can't cut any sweet. What's the fairest way to do it — and is 'fair' the same as 'equal'?"

**What a strong response looks like:** Recognises 8 doesn't divide by 3, proposes a scheme (2 each, then rotate/save/decide the last two), and engages with the deeper point that *fair* might mean equal, or by need, or by taking turns.

**What a weak response looks like:** Freezes at "it doesn't divide" with no attempt, or ignores the fairness question entirely.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"What if one child hadn't had any breakfast?"*
- *"Could 'fair' ever mean giving people different amounts?"*

**What to listen for:** Comfort with a problem that has no clean answer, and the leap from arithmetic to the idea of fairness.

---

### B3 — Why Is the Sky Dark at Night

> "The Sun is incredibly bright. There are billions of stars in the sky. So why is the sky dark at night rather than blazing with light? Have a go — I don't expect a perfect answer."

**What a strong response looks like:** Any genuine attempt to reason — stars are very far away, light spreads out, the Sun is on the other side of the Earth, space is enormous. Building an argument step by step, not reciting a fact.

**What a weak response looks like:** "Because it's night" with no reasoning, or refusing to attempt it.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"If a star is so far away, what happens to its light on the way here?"*
- *"What would the sky look like if the stars were much closer?"*

**What to listen for:** Willingness to reason about something unfamiliar. The point is the attempt and the chain of thought, not the physics.

---

### B4 — The Bat and the Ball

> "A bat and a ball cost £1.10 together. The bat costs £1 more than the ball. How much does the ball cost? Take your time and think out loud."

**What a strong response looks like:** Notices the tempting-but-wrong answer (10p) and checks it (£1.10 + 10p = £1.20, too much), then reasons to 5p, or sets it up carefully. Crucially: *checks* their answer.

**What a weak response looks like:** Blurts "10p" and moves on without checking, or gives up immediately.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Can you check that for me?"*
- *"What made the first answer tempting?"*

**What to listen for:** Do they slow down and verify rather than grab the obvious answer? Self-correction is a strong signal.

---

### B5 — Design a Test

> "How would you work out how many piano tuners there are in London — without looking it up? You'll have to estimate. Walk me through it."

**What a strong response looks like:** Breaks it into steps — roughly how many people, how many have pianos, how often they're tuned, how many a tuner does in a day — and multiplies/divides sensibly. Comfortable with rough numbers.

**What a weak response looks like:** "I don't know, you'd have to count them," with no attempt to break the problem down.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Roughly how many people live in London — just a sensible guess?"*
- *"Does every home have a piano?"*

**What to listen for:** Structured estimation and comfort with approximation. This tests whether they can build a model from nothing.

---

## SECTION C — Extracurricular Interests

*Passion, initiative and reflection. Look for depth in one or two things, self-driven learning, and the ability to reflect on progress and setbacks — not a long list of clubs.*

---

### C1 — What You Love Outside Class

> "What do you most enjoy doing when you're not in lessons?"

**What a strong response looks like:** Genuine enthusiasm for one or two things, with detail and a reason they love it — not a list. "I love drawing comics because I get to invent whole worlds."

**What a weak response looks like:** A rattled-off list of clubs with no feeling, or "watching TV" with no attempt to say why or what they get from it.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"What is it about that you enjoy most?"*
- *"How did you get into it?"*

**What to listen for:** Depth over breadth. One real passion, explained with *why*, beats five hobbies listed flatly.

---

### C2 — Taught Yourself Something

> "Have you ever taught yourself something without a teacher or a class? How did you go about it?"

**What a strong response looks like:** A concrete example of self-driven learning (a coding project, a card trick, cooking, a language app) and the *method* — trial and error, watching videos, practising. Initiative on display.

**What a weak response looks like:** "No, I only learn at school," or an example with no sense of how they actually did it.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"What was hard about learning it on your own?"*
- *"What would you teach someone else about it?"*

**What to listen for:** Independent initiative and a real learning process. Self-teaching is one of the strongest 11+ signals.

---

### C3 — A Skill You've Worked Hard At

> "Tell me about a skill you've really had to work at. What kept you going?"

**What a strong response looks like:** Honest account of effort over time, plateaus or frustration, and what motivated them to persist. Reflection on progress and setbacks.

**What a weak response looks like:** "I'm just naturally good at it," or a skill named with no story of effort.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Was there a moment you wanted to give up?"*
- *"How do you know you've improved?"*

**What to listen for:** Grit and self-reflection. The setbacks are the interesting part — draw them out.

---

### C4 — Teach Me Something

> "Teach me something you know well that I probably don't — anything at all. Take your time."

**What a strong response looks like:** Chooses something they genuinely understand and explains it clearly, checking the listener follows. Enthusiasm and clarity together.

**What a weak response looks like:** Picks something and then can't explain it, or a flat recitation of facts with no structure.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Why did you choose that?"*
- *"What's the trickiest bit for a beginner?"*

**What to listen for:** Can they organise an explanation and gauge whether the listener understands? Teaching reveals real mastery.

---

## SECTION D — Current Affairs & Moral Thinking

*Awareness, empathy and independent thought. Reward a reasoned point of view, willingness to consider another side, and originality — not a "correct" opinion.*

---

### D1 — Something in the News

> "Tell me about something happening in the world at the moment that you think is important. What's going on, and what do you make of it?"

**What a strong response looks like:** A specific story (not a vague topic), explained accurately in their own words, with a genuine opinion — including what puzzles or troubles them about it.

**What a weak response looks like:** A vague topic ("climate change") with no specific event, or an opinion with no facts behind it.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Who's most affected by this?"*
- *"Is there anything about it you don't fully understand yet?"* — noticing your own gaps is impressive

**What to listen for:** Do they separate fact from opinion, and say "I think" when it's a view? Intellectual honesty over confident bluffing.

---

### D2 — Should Children Have Phones

> "Do you think children your age should have smartphones? Why, or why not?"

**What a strong response looks like:** A clear view with reasons, *and* acknowledgement of the other side ("they're useful for safety, but they can be distracting and…"). Weighs benefits against harms.

**What a weak response looks like:** "Yes because they're fun" with no reasoning, or refusing to commit to any view.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"What would you say to someone who disagrees?"*
- *"Should there be any rules? Who should decide them?"*

**What to listen for:** Balanced reasoning and willingness to hold two sides at once, rather than a one-sided opinion.

---

### D3 — Is It Ever Right to Break a Rule

> "Is it ever right to break a rule? Can you think of an example?"

**What a strong response looks like:** Distinguishes rules from what's *right*, offers a real example (helping someone in danger, an unfair rule), and reasons about when breaking a rule might be justified — while seeing why rules matter.

**What a weak response looks like:** "Rules are always right" or "You can break any rule you want," with no nuance either way.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"What's the difference between a rule and doing the right thing?"*
- *"Who gets to decide when a rule is unfair?"*

**What to listen for:** Moral maturity — holding "rules keep us safe" and "some rules are wrong" together, with reasons.

---

### D4 — A Fair Decision

> "Imagine you're in charge of your school for a day and you can change one rule to make things fairer. What would you change, and who would it help?"

**What a strong response looks like:** A specific change, a reason it's fairer, and thought about who benefits and whether anyone loses out. Empathy plus consequences.

**What a weak response looks like:** "No homework ever," said only because it benefits them, with no fairness reasoning.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Would anyone be worse off if you changed that?"*
- *"How would you know if it actually worked?"*

**What to listen for:** Fairness reasoning that considers others, not just self-interest — and awareness of trade-offs.

---

### D5 — A Robot That Could Do Any Job

> "Suppose someone invents a robot that can do any human job perfectly. Would that be a good thing or a bad thing? Think it through."

**What a strong response looks like:** Weighs both sides — freeing people from dull or dangerous work vs jobs and purpose lost — and reaches a considered view, perhaps "it depends how we use it."

**What a weak response looks like:** "Good, because robots are cool" or "Bad, robots are scary," with no reasoning.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"What might people do with their time instead?"*
- *"Who decides who benefits from the robots?"*

**What to listen for:** Nuanced thinking about a big, open question — consequences, fairness, and a willingness to sit with "it depends."
